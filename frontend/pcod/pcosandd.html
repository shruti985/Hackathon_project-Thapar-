What are PCOD & PCOS?

PCOD (Polycystic Ovarian Disease)

Ovaries produce immature or partially mature eggs that can form small cysts over time. Leads to hormonal imbalance but is usually milder and often responds well to lifestyle changes.

Common signs: irregular cycles, weight gain, acne/oily skin, hair thinning, fatigue.

PCOS (Polycystic Ovary Syndrome)

A broader hormonal & metabolic disorder with higher androgens. Affects multiple systems and can have significant long-term impacts. Often needs medical treatment in addition to lifestyle changes.

Common signs: severe cycle issues, hirsutism, persistent acne, weight challenges, infertility, higher risk of diabetes/heart disease.

Symptoms: Overlap & Differences

Both can share symptoms (e.g., irregular periods, acne). However, PCOS tends to be more severe and is more strongly linked with metabolic problems like insulin resistance.

Shared

  • Irregular or delayed periods
  • Acne, oily skin
  • Weight gain
  • Mood changes

More PCOD-typical

  • Milder hormonal imbalance
  • Symptoms often improve with lifestyle
  • Fertility usually preserved

More PCOS-typical

  • Marked hirsutism/persistent acne
  • Infertility risk, anovulation
  • Insulin resistance & metabolic syndrome

Get Timely Care

When to Visit a Doctor

  • Frequent missed/irregular periods
  • Rapid or unexplained weight gain
  • Severe acne, excess facial/body hair
  • Pelvic pain or prolonged/heavy bleeding
  • Difficulty conceiving
  • Signs of insulin resistance (e.g., dark velvety skin patches)

Care Pathways

Treatment Options

PCOD

  • Balanced diet; reduce refined sugar
  • Regular exercise & weight management
  • Stress reduction (yoga, meditation)
  • Cycle-regulating meds as needed

PCOS

  • Lifestyle measures as above
  • Hormonal therapy to manage androgens
  • Address insulin resistance (as advised)
  • Fertility support where required

FAQs

1) What's the core difference between PCOD and PCOS?

PCOD is primarily an ovarian issue with milder hormonal changes; PCOS is a broader endocrine/metabolic syndrome with higher androgens and systemic effects.

2) Are the symptoms similar?

Some overlap (irregular periods, acne), but PCOS symptoms are typically more severe and longer-lasting.

3) Can PCOD turn into PCOS?

Noâ€”these are distinct conditions. Both benefit from early lifestyle management and medical guidance.

4) How is treatment different?

PCOD often improves with lifestyle alone; PCOS usually needs combined lifestyle + medical therapy.
